Big Pine, California

Background

Big Pine was established in 1870 with the first U.S. post office opening and it is located 4 hours north of Los Angeles. Right in between the Western Sierra Nevada Mountains and east of the White Mountains, Big Pine is surrounded by beautiful mountains with ice caps (glaciers).

Big Pine California is located north of Lone Pine California, which is the town next to tallest mountain peak in the contiguous United States, Mount whitney, at a staggering 14,505 feet, and south of Bishop California.

With a population of of 1,800, Big Pine California is right in the middle of some of the best hiking, fishing, camping and overall outdoor areas in the state of California. The town is a regular stop for truckers traveling across the state because of the great food and service it has as well as a thriving hospitality business.

Historical Significance

The Roosevelt Tree was planted in 1921 and cut down in 2020 due to various problems including disease and contamination. As the town had kept this tree as a symbol of the past, Big Pine Civic Club decided to plant four new Pine trees in honor of the memories of three Big Pine natives, Bob Steele, Cindy Schlick and Avo Nersesian as well as the historical Native American Paiute Tribe of the Owens Valley. All three of these individuals had done a great service to the town of Big Pine through their lives as well as the Paiute Tribe, setting a landmark supreme court decision victory that would go on to be cited as caselaw in hundreds, if not, thousands of cases for fair and equal treatment of Americans.

Los Angeles gets its water from Big Pine

As many of us know, Los Angeles is a very dry place and we get water from different parts of the United States. The Los Angeles Department of Water and Power has accomplished this task by engineering lakes and aqueducts from the Palisades Mountain range through creating various lakes thriving with wildlife.

North Lake is one of those Lakes that the LADWP has built for such a task. As the glaciers melt during the warmer seasons, water is trapped in the man made lake. The water is slowly released from one lake to another via controlled streams where eventually it will get to the base of the Owens valley and make its journey downsouth to Los Angeles.
